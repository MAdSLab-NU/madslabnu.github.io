Research

Decoding the molecular machinery of bacterial outer membrane biogenesis.

Overview

The MAdS LAB investigates the biochemical principles of membrane protein biogenesis in bacterial cells. We focus on the outer membrane (OM) of Gram-negative bacteria, where a conserved multiprotein complex — the β-barrel assembly machine (BAM) — folds and inserts essential transmembrane proteins. By combining genetics, biochemistry, and structural biology, we seek to define the mechanistic rules of assembly and understand how this process can be disrupted by naturally evolved inhibitors.

Research Areas

Area 1 — Mechanisms of β-barrel Assembly

How does the BAM complex fold and insert hundreds of structurally diverse β-barrel proteins into the bacterial outer membrane? Using genetic, biochemical, and structural approaches — including cryo-electron microscopy — our lab investigates the molecular rules governing this essential and conserved process. We are particularly interested in understanding how BAM catalyzes the assembly of structurally diverse substrates and how different regions of the BAM machine coordinate to promote β-barrel biogenesis.

Area 2 — Bacteriocins as Probes of Outer Membrane Biogenesis

Bacteriocins are narrow-spectrum antimicrobial proteins that selectively kill bacteria by targeting the BAM complex. Our lab studies how these naturally evolved toxins inhibit β-barrel assembly, revealing both fundamental principles of membrane biogenesis and potential strategies for targeted antibacterial development. By defining the molecular determinants of bacteriocin selectivity, we seek to uncover new insights into how outer membrane assembly can be perturbed and controlled.

Area 3 — (Meta)genome Mining for Novel Natural Products

Bacterial genomes encode a vast and largely untapped reservoir of natural products. Our lab uses computational and experimental approaches to mine bacterial (meta)genomes for novel bacteriocins and other antimicrobial compounds, with a focus on identifying molecules that target outer membrane biogenesis. This work bridges genomics, chemical biology, and microbiology to discover new chemical tools for studying and manipulating bacterial physiology.
